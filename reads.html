What is this page? Glad you asked. My sophomore fall I took a Cybersecurity Ethics course. One of the most impactful readings I engaged in was this one by John Naughton. Naughton writes that, “the new masters of our universe are people who are essentially only half-educated,” and that if they had been fully educated, they may have had more foresight onto the problems that could arise from their technologies. As someone who is working toward a STEM-focused undergraduate education, I recognize that one of my limitations is that I will not be able to take as many humanities and ethics courses. Thus, I’ve made it my responsibility to self educate myself on these topics. Here are the books I've gotten the most value from while attending college, categorized by genre, accomapnied with a (hopefully) intriguing description.

Currently working through...

  • Don Quixote by Cervantes
  • Rich Dad Poor Dad by Robert Kiyosaki
  • The Heart of the Buddha's Teaching by Thich Nhat Hanh

Philsophy, Ethics, and History

  • Justice by Michael Sandel: an honest discussion of life's dilemmas, analyzed through various ethical frameworks
  • Meditations by Marcus Aurelius: personal thoughts and affirmations of one of the greatest leaders in history
  • Goodbye, Things: The New Japanese Minimalism by Fumio Sasaki: a reflection on Sasaki's minimalist experience

Societal Issues

  • The Autobiography of Malcolm X by Malcolm X and Alex Haley: insight into the life of man whose ideas were beyond his time
  • So You Want to Talk About Race by Iejoma Oluo: a relevant, digestable piece on racial injustice and systemic racism

Classics

  • The Catcher in the Rye by J.D. Salinger: a self-searching protagonist who is equal parts relatable and unrelatable

Personal Development

  • Talking to Strangers by Malcolm Gladwell: humans are really bad at interacting with strangers
  • How to Win Friends & Influence People by Dale Carnegie: humans can get better at interacting with strangers by following Carnegie's advice
