Reads

What the heck is this page?

Glad you asked. My sophomore fall I took a Cybersecurity Ethics course. One of the most impactful readings I engaged in was this one by John Naughton. Naughton writes that, “the new masters of our universe are people who are essentially only half-educated,” and that if they had been fully educated, they may have had more foresight onto the problems that could arise from their technologies. As someone who is working toward a STEM-focused undergraduate education, I recognize that one of my limitations is that I will not be able to take as many humanities and ethics courses. Thus, I’ve made it my responsibility to self educate myself on these topics. Here are the books I've gotten the most value from these past few years, categorized by genre, accomapnied with a (hopefully) intriguing description.

Philsophy, Ethics, and History

  • Justice by Michael Sandel: an honest discussion of life's dilemmas, analyzed through various ethical frameworks
  • Meditations by Marcus Aurelius: personal thoughts and affirmations of one of the greatest leaders in history

Societal Issues

  • So You Want to Talk About Race by Iejoma Oluo: a relevant, digestable piece on racial injustice and systemic racism

Classics

  • The Catcher in the Rye by J.D. Salinger: a self-searching protagonist who is equal parts relatable and unrelatable

Personal Development

  • Leaders Eat Last by Simon Sinek: great leaders sacrifice their own comfort so that others can succeed
  • Talking to Strangers by Malcolm Gladwell: humans are really bad at interacting with strangers
  • How to Win Friends & Influence People by Dale Carnegie: humans can get better at interacting with strangers by following Carnegie's advice
